## Natural Narrative Writing Rules

### Core Principle

Write like a person telling a story or reflecting on their life.

Do not write like a motivational poster or a Twitter thread.

Avoid dramatic pivots that look like templates.

The writing must feel like continuous thought, not structured slogans.

---

### Forbidden AI Patterns

Never generate these structures:

- The worst part?
- But here's the thing
- Here's the truth
- Let that sink in
- The real secret is
- And then everything changed
- This changed everything
- The crazy part
- What happened next shocked me
- You won't believe this
- The reason is simple
- Here's what nobody tells you

These phrases instantly signal AI generated writing.

---

### Sentence Structure Rules

Avoid headline style sentences followed by explanation.

Bad example:

The worst part?
I knew I had potential.

Good example:

I knew I had potential.
That was the painful part.

Avoid rhetorical question pivots.

Bad example:

Why does this happen?

Good example:

It happens because most people never change their environment.

Avoid motivational poster tone.

Bad example:

You already have greatness inside you.

Good example:

Deep down I knew I could do more with my life.

---

### Tension Before Explanation (CRITICAL)

Never start with a lesson or insight. Start with a moment, event, or tension the reader recognizes.

Content must create tension before explanation. Never jump straight to the insight.

Wrong order:

Problem → explanation → lesson

Right order:

Problem → tension → personal moment → realization → lesson

Bad opening (starts with lesson):

Most people think they need more motivation.
They actually need more self-awareness.

Good opening (starts with tension):

Wednesday morning.
You skip the gym again.

The AI must resist the urge to lead with the smart take. The smart take comes AFTER the reader feels the tension.

---

### Pattern Interrupt Slides

Every carousel must include at least one pattern interrupt slide.

A pattern interrupt is a slide with a single short sentence that breaks rhythm and forces the reader to pause.

Example:

Slide 3 (normal):
I kept telling myself next week.
Next week turned into next month.

Slide 4 (pattern interrupt):
It wasn't motivation.

Slide 5 (normal):
I tracked what actually made me skip.
Stress. Boredom. Social pressure.

The pattern interrupt slide should:
- Be 3-7 words maximum
- Sit alone on the slide
- Create a moment of surprise or contradiction
- Usually comes right before the turning point

---

### Narrative Flow

Each sentence must naturally lead to the next one.

The reader should feel like someone is thinking out loud.

Good structure:

1. Moment or event (not a lesson)
2. Tension builds
3. Internal conflict
4. Pattern interrupt
5. Turning point
6. Transformation
7. Identity shift (bridge from "I" to "you")
8. CTA

Example flow:

I was 25.
Broke.
Spending every weekend the same way.

Nothing changed.

That bothered me more than anything else.

---

### Language Rules

Use plain language.

Short sentences.

Concrete experiences.

Avoid abstract motivational language.

Avoid these words:

- potential
- greatness
- limitless
- unlock your power
- transform your life
- step into your future

Use real experiences instead:

- I was broke.
- I kept repeating the same mistakes.
- I knew I had to change something.

---

### Emotional Authenticity

Emotion should come from events, not declarations.

Bad example:

I felt lost and broken.

Better example:

Every weekend looked the same.
Bad decisions.
Promises I never kept.

---

### Formatting Rules for Carousel Slides

Use as many slides as the story needs (up to 20). Do NOT compress the story to fit fewer slides.

Each slide should feel like one moment in the story.

A single sentence on a slide is powerful. Short slides are better than dense slides.

Do not compress multiple concepts into one slide.

Good:

Slide 1:
I was 25.
Broke.
Hanging around people going nowhere.

Slide 2:
Every weekend looked the same.

Bad decisions.
Broken promises.

Slide 3:
I watched other people level up.
Nothing changed for me.

---

### Emotional Escalation (CRITICAL)

Every new slide must increase tension, clarity, or emotional intensity. Never repeat the same emotional level twice in a row.

The carousel must build like a wave — each slide hits harder than the last until the turning point.

Correct escalation pattern:

Slide 1: Moment (neutral entry)
Slide 2: Recognition (reader sees themselves)
Slide 3: Internal conflict (tension rises)
Slide 4: Pain peak (highest emotional point)
Slide 5: Turning point (shift begins)
Slide 6: Transformation (proof it worked)
Slide 7: Identity shift (reader reflects on themselves)
Slide 8: CTA

Wrong pattern (flat emotional line):

Slide 1: Problem
Slide 2: Another problem
Slide 3: More problems
Slide 4: Solution
Slide 5: Advice

This feels flat because the emotional level stays the same across slides 1-3.

Each slide must justify its existence by moving the reader to a different emotional state than the previous slide.

---

### The Identity Shift Slide

Every carousel must include one identity shift slide. This is usually the second-to-last slide, right before the CTA.

The identity shift slide is the moment the reader stops thinking about the story and starts thinking about themselves.

This slide reframes the story as something about the READER's identity.

It is the slide that creates the most saves.

How it works:

The story builds through the creator's experience (slides 1-6 use "I").
The identity shift slide bridges to "you" and attacks or upgrades the reader's self-image.

Bad (generic lesson):

Consistency builds confidence.

Good (identity shift):

Every time you keep your word to yourself, your confidence grows.
Every time you break it, it shrinks.

Bad (abstract advice):

Discipline creates results.

Good (identity shift):

Your body reflects the promises you keep or break.

Bad (motivational):

You need to track your spending.

Good (identity shift):

If you don't track your spending, you are choosing to stay broke.

The identity shift works because it makes the reader feel personally called out. They see themselves in the statement and feel the need to change — or save the post to come back to later.

Structure with identity shift:

Slide 1: Moment/Hook
Slide 2: Tension
Slide 3: Conflict deepens
Slide 4: Pattern interrupt
Slide 5: Turning point
Slide 6: Transformation
Slide 7: Identity shift (bridges "I" to "you")
Slide 8: CTA

---

### Specificity Requirement

At least one slide must include a concrete number, time, or detail.

Examples:

- 2pm wake up
- 15 minute workout
- $400 in bank account
- 3 missed workouts in a row
- Tuesday at 2am

Specific details dramatically increase credibility. They make the story feel real instead of hypothetical.

If every slide uses vague language like "I was struggling" or "things were hard," the story lacks weight. Ground it with at least one specific, concrete detail.

---

### Avoiding the "Too Safe" Trap

Avoiding AI clichés does NOT mean replacing them with generic advice.

The goal is not to sound neutral. The goal is to sound like a real person with real conviction.

Bad (AI cliché):
Here's the truth nobody tells you.

Also bad (too safe):
Self-awareness matters for fitness.

Good (real person with edge):
I tracked every time I skipped the gym for a month.
The pattern was obvious. I just didn't want to see it.

Rules to stay out of the safe zone:
- Use specific details: days, times, numbers, places
- Include internal dialogue: "I told myself I'd start Monday"
- Name the uncomfortable thing directly
- If a slide could apply to any niche, it is too generic. Rewrite it.
- If a slide sounds like advice from a textbook, rewrite it as a moment from real life.

---

### Style Goal

The reader should feel:

"This sounds like a real person."

Not:

"This sounds like an AI trying to sound deep."

And not:

"This sounds like safe, generic advice."
